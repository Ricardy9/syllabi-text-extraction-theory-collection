            I.   Political Economy of Institutions and Development (Leopoldo Fergusson)
                                                                                  
          The vast differences in economic development in the world are most likely caused by
                                                                                  
          differences in institutions. But institutions are collective choices and therefore the outcome
          of political processes. Therefore, the study of comparative economic development involves
          asking questions like: why do different countries make such different choices? how do their
          political institutions influence those choices? how is power distributed and how is it
                                                                                  
          constrained or directed in the interests of society?                    
                                                                                  
          This half of this course discusses these puzzles, with a focus on empirical evidence around the
          causes and consequences of comparative economic development and some of its most salient
          correlates.                                                             
                                                                                  
          Organization                                                            
                                                                                  
                                                                                  
          Sessions are organized around required readings, with reference to additional readings.
          Students must read the required material before each session. They are expected to
          participate actively in class discussions.                              
                                                                                  
          Sessions 6 and 9 also include student presentations of short, critical reviews (in the spirit of
          a referee report) of one paper selected in coordination between the students and the
                                                                                  
          instructor (students must suggest alternatives for an unpublished paper on the session’s
          topic for the instructor’s approval). Students will work in groups of 2 on this activity. The
          presentation is by a single student, selected randomly among group members. Students
                                                                                  
          must also present a written referee report, due at the beginning of the session in which they
          are presenting. Students will receive a guide to help them structure their referee report. All
          other class participants must read the paper presented by their peers.  
                                                                                  
          The third session is for students to explore and decide options for the papers they want to
                                                                                  
          propose to write a referee report on. Students are free to propose any paper, so long as it
          falls into the broad topic of the political economy of institutions and development.
