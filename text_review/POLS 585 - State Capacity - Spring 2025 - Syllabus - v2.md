                               Course  Description                                
                                                                                  
          The concept of state capacity refers to the government’s ability to function effectively
          and achieve its policy objectives, such as raising revenue or enforcing law and order.
          This course provides a comprehensive and detailed survey of the academic literature
          on state capacity, with a strong emphasis on the inner workings of government and
          the role of bureaucracy. This course is aimed at PhD students in political science and
          related fields. Topics covered include political institutions, the role of war, measurement
          issues, recruitment and incentives of public employees, and organizational structure. The
                                                                                  
          course combines formal models and empirical evidence from historical (e.g., Europe, US,
          China) and contemporary settings (developed and developing countries). The readings
          will include classic texts as well as recent research in political science and economics.
                                                                                  
          Quotidian Course Structure. Students are expected to carefully read all assigned
          readings prior to class. Each week we will be reading approximately 100 pages. We will
          discuss each reading as a group, without a predetermined presenter, although sometimes
                                                                                  
          I will take the lead. You must come prepared to class. There is nowhere to hide! After
          the first few weeks, we will devote the first minutes of class to open discussion of research
          ideas, progress, and difficulties. We will also have a shared class journal in the form
          of a Google Doc where we summarize the papers we read and our weekly discussions.
          Responsibility for these weekly entries will be allocated among the students.
                                                                                  
          Grading and Evaluation. In a PhD program, grades are largely irrelevant. Pretty
          much no one will ever see them and they will have close to null influence on future
                                                                                  
          academic and career progress. Your main incentive should be to make the most of this
          valuable opportunity to learn in great detail about one topic and to make use of this
          knowledge in your own research. Class preparation and participation should be your
          main focus in this class. As such, this represents 60% of your grade. Everyone will start
          with an A in this component and I will adjust downward if needed, letting the student
          know when this happens. At the same time, you should be thinking about research all the
          time. This doesn’t mean stressing out about how many papers you have written or how
